How this works

The model behind the calculator, in plain language. Seven short sections, one idea each.

01 · The idea

Insurers average. You can't.

Insurers price risk arithmetically — the law of large numbers works across thousands of policies. A single business can't. It faces one path, once. That gap is where this sits.

02 · The base

Surplus cash is the base.

W = surplus cash

The only asset that reliably absorbs a loss. Property and equipment are excluded, because the event that destroys them is often the one you'd need them to absorb.

03 · The climb

Recovery gets steeper, then vertical.

climb = L / (W − L)

To recover a loss L from a base W, what's left must grow by L / (W − L). Lose 10%, grow 11%. Lose half, it must double. Lose 90%, it must grow tenfold. The cost of recovery curves, then goes vertical.

04 · The felt cost

Money's value depends on how much you have.

felt cost = ln(W / (W − L))

Bernoulli, 1738: money's value is its usefulness, which depends on how much you already have. As L approaches W, the felt cost tends to infinity.

05 · The revenue climb

Cash rebuilds out of sales.

extra sales = L / margin

Cash rebuilds out of sales, and only a slice of each dollar survives costs. Margin comes from your figures. Because it ignores tax and fixed costs, it's a floor.

06 · What's deliberately absent

No probability. No time horizon. No assumed margin.

Each would need a number the model has no honest way to know.

07 · Source

One source, and an old one.

Bernoulli, D. (1738), Exposition of a New Theory on the Measurement of Risk.
