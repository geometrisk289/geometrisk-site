Their line, from three numbers they already know.

A $95k loss climbs back at +17% of what's left — felt, but survivable. A loss near the full $650k has no base left to grow from. Everything below sets where retention should sit; everything approaching the base is what transfer is for.

Two independent moves, one direction: retain more of the high-frequency layer the business can shrug off, and transfer more of the low-frequency tail that would ruin it. Cheaper and safer are not a trade-off here — they're the same move.

How to read this
  • Illustrative only. Northbridge Joinery is a fictional business. The figures show the shape of the method, not a quote or a recommendation.
  • Surplus cash is the base. Property and equipment are excluded — the event that destroys them is often the one you'd need them to absorb.
  • Retention is set at the absorbable line, not below it. The line comes from the client's own numbers; the placement may land at or under it, never above.
  • The spend figure is a total, judged against what it removes. A lower number that leaves the tail exposed is not a saving.
  • The full derivation — the base, the climb, the felt cost — is in the Risk Library.
